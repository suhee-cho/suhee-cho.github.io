Welcome, folks!

I’m Suhee, a post-baccalaureate researcher advised by Dr. Jay McClelland at Stanford Psychology. Before joining Jay’s lab, I was an undergraduate student at KAIST, studying bio & brain engineering and biological sciences.

My research interests center on how we learn and form memories In more technical terms, how we build internal models of the external world, how these representations are maintained, and how they are used to guide adaptive behavior. I explore these questions primarily through connectionist models (Yes, I have a small artificial brain, coded by me, running on my computer! ). You can dig further into my recent projects on my project page.

Beyond research, I enjoy writing Not only about science, but also about life and the random thoughts. For anybody who is interested, I share some of them on my blog page. Other than that, I love staying active! Living in the San Francisco Bay Area means easy access to beautiful hiking trails and parks! I enjoy hiking, running, roller skating, and skateboarding. I’ve also been doing CrossFit for over four years.

Publications

  1. An updated integrative theory of the cognitive map and its neural implementation capturing rapid neural and behavioral adaptation
    Suhee Cho and James L McClelland
    Dec 2025
    Abs

    ABSTRACT Animals rapidly and flexibly adapt their behavior to their environment, and this is thought to depend on the formation of an internal cognitive map. The cognitive map has been studied at multiple levels of description, from abstract theory to neural implementation, with a focus on the role of learned representations in the hippocampus. Here, we (1) extend the seminal successor representation theory of the cognitive map, weighting it by factors collectively labeled perceived salience and using it to predict environmental features encountered at different places in the environment and (2) employ behavioral time-scale synaptic plasticity (BTSP), a form of rapid neural adaptation, to capture rapid map formation and behavioral adaptation. In the resulting model, hippocampal area CA3 uses temporally symmetric BTSP to encode spatiotemporal proximity experienced during exploration, area CA1 uses temporally asymmetric BTSP to encode a perceived salience-weighted successor representation, and downstream areas learn to predict environmental features that can guide behavior in accordance with a animal’s needs and goals. Through simulations of a rodent exploring a maze, we show that our model can learn this representation rapidly, and further strengthen it through spontaneous replay activity during subsequent offline periods. Predictions of environmental features also develop rapidly, and behavioral simulations show how these learned representations give rise to need-dependent choice, rapid behavioral adaptation to changes in the availability of reward, and one-trial shock avoidance learning.

  2. Neuromimetic metaplasticity for adaptive continual learning without catastrophic forgetting
    Suhee Cho, Hyeonsu Lee, Seungdae Baek, and 1 more author
    Neural Netw., Oct 2025
    Abs

    Conventional intelligent systems based on deep neural network (DNN) models encounter challenges in achieving human-like continual learning due to catastrophic forgetting. Here, we propose a metaplasticity model inspired by human working memory, enabling DNNs to perform catastrophic forgetting-resistant continual learning without any pre- or post-processing. A key aspect of our approach involves implementing distinct types of synapses from stable to flexible, and randomly intermixing them to train synaptic connections with different degrees of flexibility. This strategy allowed the network to successfully learn a continuous stream of information, even under unexpected changes in input length. The model achieved a balanced tradeoff between memory capacity and performance without requiring additional training or structural modifications, dynamically allocating memory resources to retain both old and new information. Furthermore, the model demonstrated robustness against data poisoning attacks by selectively filtering out erroneous memories, leveraging the Hebb repetition effect to reinforce the retention of significant data.

  3. Exploring the architectural biases of the cortical microcircuit
    Aishwarya Balwani, Suhee Cho, and Hannah Choi
    Neural Comput., Aug 2025
    Abs

    The cortex plays a crucial role in various perceptual and cognitive functions, driven by its basic unit, the canonical cortical microcircuit. Yet, we remain short of a framework that definitively explains the structure-function relationships of this fundamental neuroanatomical motif. To better understand how physical substrates of cortical circuitry facilitate their neuronal dynamics, we employ a computational approach using recurrent neural networks and representational analyses. We examine the differences manifested by the inclusion and exclusion of biologically motivated interareal laminar connections on the computational roles of different neuronal populations in the microcircuit of hierarchically related areas throughout learning. Our findings show that the presence of feedback connections correlates with the functional modularization of cortical populations in different layers and provides the microcircuit with a natural inductive bias to differentiate expected and unexpected inputs at initialization, which we justify mathematically. Furthermore, when testing the effects of training the microcircuit and its variants with a predictive-coding-inspired strategy, we find that doing so helps better encode noisy stimuli in areas of the cortex that receive feedback, all of which combine to suggest evidence for a predictive-coding mechanism serving as an intrinsic operative logic in the cortex.
